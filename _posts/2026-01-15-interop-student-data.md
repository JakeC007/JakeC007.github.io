title: Student Learning Data Needs a Commons, Not a Cage

---

Cory Doctorow's framework of enshittification describes a reliable platform lifecycle: serve users first, then squeeze them to serve business customers, then extract value from everyone until the whole thing decays. I have spent the last few years studying K-12 EdTech from a privacy and HCI perspective, and the pattern fits uncomfortably well.

The privacy problem and the interoperability problem are usually treated as separate policy issues. I think that framing understates how much they reinforce each other. When student learning data lives in proprietary formats that no other system can read, it becomes practically impossible to audit, port, or delete. A child who changes schools, or whose district switches vendors at contract renewal, loses years of learning history. A teacher cannot pull diagnostic data from one platform into the tool where they plan instruction. The opacity is not incidental. It is structural, and it serves vendor interests by making districts expensive to leave.

In a working paper exploring data trusts as a governance model for K-12 student data, I argue that what is missing is not just better privacy law but a different ownership architecture. A data trust would place student data under independent stewardship, held on behalf of students and families rather than controlled by platforms, with clear rules about what vendors can access, for how long, and for what purposes. The analogy to other sectors is instructive: FHIR in healthcare and Open Banking in finance both demonstrate that interoperability and accountability are compatible with a functioning vendor ecosystem. 

This points toward an open learner data standard: versioned, community-governed, freely implementable, and written into procurement contracts so that districts have real leverage at the table. Interoperability alone does not solve the deeper governance problem. But without it, even well-designed oversight frameworks are operating with incomplete information, because the data itself remains invisible inside systems that are not required to be legible to anyone but their owners.

The students in these systems did not choose to have their learning histories locked in proprietary platforms. Building the infrastructure that gives them, and the schools that serve them, some meaningful say over that data is a reasonable place to start.
