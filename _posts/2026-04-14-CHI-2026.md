When we hear about online scams, we often focus on financial loss. Our paper, *“It didn’t feel right but I needed a job so desperately”: Understanding People’s Emotions and Help Needs During Scams*, looks beyond that outcome. It examines why people engage with specific scams and what kinds of support they need at different stages to prevent, diagnose, mitigate, and recover from harm.

### How We Did It

To understand the in-the-moment experience of being targeted, we turned to Reddit, where thousands of people share real-time accounts of scam encounters. We focused on targets’ emotional states at key moments, such as when they are first drawn in by a scammer, when they begin to suspect something is wrong, and when harm occurs.

Given the volume of data, we used a Large Language Model (LLM) to identify candidate help-seeking posts. This was only a starting point. Our research team then conducted careful human review and filtering to ensure quality and relevance. The result is a dataset of 405 detailed accounts across 12 scam types, which allowed us to systematically map the emotional trajectory of an active scam.

### The Five Emotional "Hooks"

Our analysis shows that scammers manipulate five core emotions to bypass skepticism and drive compliance. Each plays a distinct role:

* **Hope:** Scammers exploit a need for stable income or basic necessities, leading targets to overlook warning signs.
* **Fear:** Scammers create high-stakes scenarios that trigger panic and reduce a target’s ability to think critically.
* **Guilt and Goodness:** Scammers appeal to a target’s desire to help or to see themselves as a good person. They construct situations where inaction appears harmful, leveraging a sense of responsibility or duty.
* **Trust:** Scammers take advantage of trust in familiar platforms or authority figures. By mimicking legitimate processes, they make fraudulent interactions feel routine and safe.
* **Belonging:** Scammers tap into the need for connection by offering companionship or a sense of community.

### Why It Matters

Viewing scams only as technical or financial failures overlooks their human impact. Targets often experience shame, social withdrawal, and even symptoms associated with trauma. Falling for a scam is not a reflection of intelligence. It reflects how effectively scammers exploit a person’s immediate circumstances.

Our goal is to inform the design of timely and effective interventions that align with people’s needs at different stages of a scam.

### What We Can Do

Current prevention strategies often rely on deficit-based education, such as telling people to “spot the red flags.” Our research instead maps help-seeking needs, including Sensemaking, Guidance, Therapeutic support, and External Action, onto the scam lifecycle to support more effective, evidence-based defenses.

We propose “just-in-time” interventions that meet people where they are:

* **The Right Message:** Interventions should be emotionally aware. Warnings need to address the specific emotion being targeted, such as acknowledging the urgency of finding a job rather than simply blocking a link.
* **The Right Need:** When something feels off during the diagnostic phase, people need support for sensemaking. Platforms should clearly explain the tactic being used so users can disengage with confidence.
* **The Right Time:** Warnings should appear within the platforms where scams occur, such as job boards or dating apps, rather than being confined to external security pages.

By shifting the focus from blaming individuals to understanding their emotional experiences, we can design trauma-informed safety tools that provide support when it is most needed.
