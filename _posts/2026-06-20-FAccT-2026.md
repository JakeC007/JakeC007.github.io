When we started talking to high school students about ChatGPT, schools were still making up the rules in real time. That turned out to be the whole point. Our paper, *Investigating ChatGPT Usage in High Schools: Student Perspectives on Policy and Practice*, presented at FAccT 2026, looks at how students experienced, interpreted, and worked around their schools' attempts to govern AI in the first year it was widely available.

### Why We Did This Study

By the time we designed this study, most of the public conversation about ChatGPT in schools was about cheating. Would students use it to cut corners, and could teachers catch them? That framing left out the people most affected by it. We wanted to understand how students themselves made sense of a technology that arrived faster than any policy to govern it: what they thought was acceptable, how they read the rules, and what happened to them when those rules were unclear or unevenly enforced.

We interviewed students because they occupy an unusual position in this system. They have more autonomy than younger kids but are still governed tightly by institutional rules and adult oversight. They were also, in practice, the first people who had to decide what responsible AI use looked like, often before any adult in the building had decided for them.

### A Snapshot in Time, and Why That Still Matters

We talked to 18 students between May and August of 2023, roughly half a year after ChatGPT launched. This was a narrow window, and we want to be upfront about that. Schools had not yet standardized their policies, the dominant tool was ChatGPT rather than the many options students have now, and the "agentic" systems people worry about today were not yet in the picture.

We think this is a feature, not a limitation. We caught a natural experiment: a moment when an institution had to react to a genuinely new technology before it had settled on what to think. That early, unsettled state is exactly what makes the findings durable. The specific tool will keep changing, and it already has. But the underlying dynamic, an institution improvising governance while the technology moves faster than the policy, is not going away. If anything, it intensifies as AI gets more capable and more deeply embedded in everyday tools. The snapshot captures a pattern that keeps recurring, even as the particulars age out.

### How We Did It

We conducted semi-structured interviews with 18 students enrolled in US high schools, ranging from ninth to twelfth grade and across public, charter, magnet, and private schools. Each interview ran 50 to 75 minutes. We asked about their schools' rules around AI, how those rules were enforced, what technical monitoring they encountered, and what they thought an ideal policy would look like. We then analyzed the transcripts thematically, building a codebook from the data itself and refining it through team discussion.

### What We Found

1. **The absence of a policy did not create a vacuum. It created governance by improvisation.**
   Twelve of our 18 participants were not aware of any specific school rule about ChatGPT. Yet they were not confused about what counted as acceptable. Almost all of them independently arrived at the same line: using AI to help you think is fine, using it to replace your thinking is not. As one student put it, "There's a difference between asking for help and then just getting the straight-up answer." Nobody taught them that distinction. They generated it themselves, and roughly the same version of it across different schools.

2. **Enforcement was a patchwork, and it fell unevenly.**
   Because schools rarely had explicit AI rules, enforcement was left to individual teachers, legacy plagiarism policies, and whatever detection tool a school happened to buy. The result was that students in the same building faced opposite rules. One student's English department treated AI use on a graduation-requirement essay as serious cheating; another's teacher encouraged using it to start a draft. We borrow the term "patchwork governance" from platform-moderation research to describe this: fragmented rules, enforced differently depending on who is in the room.

3. **AI detectors failed, and not only because they were inaccurate.**
   The detectors produced false positives that students experienced as a constant background risk.[^1] One described a teacher whose checker was flagging students she had taught all year. But the deeper problem is conceptual. Detection assumes AI use is a discrete, catchable event, a moment a student goes to the chatbot and comes back with text. As AI gets built directly into the tools students write in, that clean boundary disappears, and so does the thing the detector is trying to catch.

4. **Students adapted rather than disengaged, and the most sophisticated workarounds defeated detection entirely.**
   One student described a pipeline his classmates ran, credited to TikTok: generate the essay, reword it through a paraphrasing tool, run it through a detector, and if it flagged, paraphrase again until it passed. Others simply opened ChatGPT on their phones, off the school network, where no monitoring reached them. The students the system actually caught were often the ones who showed up and did the work in the monitored environment.

### Why It Matters

The clearest lesson is that ambiguous policy is not neutral. "We're still figuring it out" sounds reasonable, and in 2023 it was, but ambiguity has a distribution. Someone absorbs the cost of an unwritten rule, and it is reliably the student with the least room to absorb it. The students who leaned on ChatGPT most, and who were most exposed when enforcement came down arbitrarily, were disproportionately from lower-resourced schools where teacher support was thin. The same uncertainty that was a minor inconvenience for a well-supported student was a genuine risk for one already behind.

It also matters that the students were not the problem the system imagined them to be. They worked out a defensible line between assistance and substitution, applied it more consistently than the adults around them managed, and largely asked to be reasoned with rather than monitored. One student emailed his teachers in advance to say he would use ChatGPT only to fix errors, and invited them to run a detector if anything looked off. He was trying to negotiate a norm with an institution that had not yet decided it had one.

### What We Can Do

We recommend that schools and districts stop treating the absence of a clear AI policy as a temporary, harmless state. The absence is itself a policy, one that pushes the hard decisions onto whoever happens to be in the room. A concrete first step is internal coherence: districts should make sure their own documents agree with each other, so that students are not asked to follow guidance that recommends a tool their school's approved list forbids.

We also recommend moving away from detection and surveillance as the primary enforcement strategy. Beyond the well-documented reliability and bias problems, detection rests on an assumption that no longer holds as AI becomes ambient in everyday software. Schools would be better served by rethinking how they evaluate learning when AI mediation is routine, and by treating students as stakeholders in setting AI norms rather than as compliance problems to be managed.

Because the technology will keep changing, the right goal is not a rule for ChatGPT specifically. It is a governance approach that survives the next tool, and the one after that. Students should not have to absorb the cost of institutional uncertainty every time the technology moves. Designing for that, rather than for the tool of the moment, is what fair AI governance in schools should mean.

[^1]: The unreliability of AI-text detection is well established across independent lines of evidence. Liang and colleagues found that detectors systematically misclassify writing by non-native English speakers as AI-generated, flagging more than half of a set of TOEFL essays as fake while scoring native-speaker essays as nearly always human, which makes the tools a fairness problem and not only an accuracy one ([Liang et al., "GPT detectors are biased against non-native English writers," *Patterns*, 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.patter.2023.100779)). Sadasivan and colleagues showed that a light paraphrasing pass breaks a wide range of detectors, including watermarking and retrieval-based schemes, and proved a theoretical result that as language models get better at imitating human text, even the best possible detector approaches a coin flip ([Sadasivan et al., "Can AI-Generated Text Be Reliably Detected?", 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.11156)). And the clearest signal came from a developer rather than a critic: OpenAI retired its own AI Text Classifier on July 20, 2023, citing its low rate of accuracy, having reported at launch that it correctly identified only 26% of AI-written text while mislabeling human text as AI 9% of the time ([OpenAI, "New AI classifier for indicating AI-written text," 2023](https://openai.com/index/new-ai-classifier-for-indicating-ai-written-text/)).
