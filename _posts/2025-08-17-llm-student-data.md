title: Students keep the copyright. The license takes everything else.

---

Under 17 U.S.C. § 102(a), copyright in an original work attaches automatically the moment that work is fixed in a tangible medium. A student submits an essay. At that instant, they are a copyright holder. The exclusive rights enumerated under 17 U.S.C. § 106 belong to them: reproduction, distribution, preparation of derivative works, public display.

What many EdTech end-user license agreements then do is extract a license so broad that the copyright, while nominally retained, offers the student almost no practical protection.

### How the license grant works

A copyright transfer must be in writing and signed to be valid. 17 U.S.C. § 204(a). But that rule applies only to transfers of ownership and to exclusive licenses. Under § 101, a non-exclusive license is not a "transfer of copyright ownership" and accordingly requires no writing, no signature, and no negotiation. Clicking "I agree" is sufficient.

This distinction is the mechanism. Platforms do not take title to student work. They take something that, for many purposes, is more useful: a non-exclusive, royalty-free, worldwide, sublicensable, irrevocable license to use it however they choose.

On one widely-used LMS platform, for instance, state that by posting user content, a user grants Instructure a "perpetual, worldwide, royalty-free, non-exclusive, sublicensable, transferable license to use, copy, translate, distribute, reproduce, modify, adapt, publicly perform and publicly display User Content in connection with our legitimate business interests." The feedback provision in the terms goes further still, granting a license to "otherwise commercially or non-commercially exploit in any manner" content submitted through the platform. These formulations are not unusual in the EdTech space. They are standard drafting.

### What ownership without protection looks like

The result is a structure in which a student technically owns their work but has licensed away the § 106 rights that make ownership meaningful. Reproduction: licensed. Derivative works: licensed. Distribution to sublicensees: licensed. The copyright remains on paper. The bundle of rights that copyright represents has been, by contract, substantially emptied.

This has always been the case to some degree with consumer software agreements. What changes the stakes is the commercial moment we are now in. The demand for high-quality, human-authored text as training data for large language models has created a market that did not exist when most of these EULA templates were written. **Student essays, submitted under compulsory institutional requirements, are precisely the kind of varied, non-synthetic human writing that commands value in that market.**

### Fair use is unsettled, and the old cases may not hold

Courts have previously extended fair use protection to EdTech platforms holding student work. In *A.V. v. iParadigms*, 562 F.3d 630 (4th Cir. 2009), the Fourth Circuit affirmed that archiving student papers in a commercial plagiarism detection database was fair use under 17 U.S.C. § 107, relying heavily on the argument that the use was transformative and caused no market harm. Institutions and platforms have treated that ruling as settling the question. It does not.

First, the broader fair use landscape is in active litigation. *The New York Times Co. v. Microsoft Corp.*, No. 1:23-cv-11195 (S.D.N.Y.), and a cluster of related cases against AI developers will likely produce the first appellate guidance on whether training large language models on copyrighted text constitutes fair use. Until those cases resolve, any confident assertion about what § 107 permits in the AI training context is premature.

Second, *iParadigms* rested on a transformativeness analysis that the Supreme Court has since substantially narrowed. In *Andy Warhol Foundation for the Visual Arts, Inc. v. Goldsmith*, 598 U.S. 508 (2023), the Court held that a use is not transformative simply because it adds new meaning or expression; the relevant question is whether the use serves a sufficiently distinct purpose from the original. A platform using student writing to develop commercial AI products is arguably serving the same expressive purpose the student did, just at scale and for profit. Whether *iParadigms* survives that analysis applied to AI training is a question no court has answered.

The fourth factor under § 107, the effect on the potential market for the copyrighted work, compounds the uncertainty. The *iParadigms* court found in 2009 that student papers had no meaningful market. That conclusion is harder to reach today. Human-authored training data has commercial value, and a market for it is developing. Courts applying *Warhol* and the emerging AI litigation framework may weigh that factor very differently than the Fourth Circuit did fifteen years ago.

**As Lawrence Lessig put it: "Fair use is nothing more than the right to hire a lawyer."**

### Schools are the ones paying. They should act like it.

There is a dimension to this that gets less attention than it deserves. Students are not the contracting party. Schools and districts are. Institutions pay for EdTech licenses, often at significant expense, and they do so on behalf of students who have no alternative but to use the tools the institution selects and mandates.

That purchasing relationship creates leverage that institutions have largely not used. A school district contracting with an EdTech vendor is not a consumer clicking through a terms page. It is a customer with bargaining power, legal counsel, and the ability to condition renewal on contractual commitments. At minimum, *institutions should be demanding explicit written assurances that student-submitted work will not be used to train AI models*, will not be sublicensed for that purpose, and will be deleted on a defined schedule. These are not exotic asks. They are the kind of data use restrictions that appear routinely in commercial contracts between sophisticated parties.

Schools and districts are navigating an overwhelming number of competing priorities, and EdTech procurement rarely sits at the top of the list. That is understandable. But institutions are the contracting party here, and that position comes with real leverage. A school district negotiating an EdTech license is not a consumer clicking through a terms page. It is a customer with bargaining power, legal counsel, and the ability to condition renewal on contractual commitments.

As AI development continues to create commercial value from the kind of text that students produce every day under compulsory submission requirements, the moment is right for institutions to ask a straightforward question at the contract stage: will our students' submitted work be used to train AI models, or sublicensed for that purpose? If the answer is no, put it in writing. If the vendor will not, that is important information too.
